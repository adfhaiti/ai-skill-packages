# Haiti Construction Context Reference

Cost benchmarks, material sourcing data, labor rates, and resilience ROI references for construction projects in Haiti. Data current as of early 2026.

## Cost Benchmarks (2025-26 USD)

### Per-Square-Foot Ranges (Engineered Residential)

| Tier | Range ($/sq ft) | What It Gets You |
|------|-----------------|------------------|
| Basic engineered | $75-90 | Confined masonry, basic finishes, corrugated metal roof, minimal MEP |
| Mid-range engineered | $90-110 | Full MEP, ceramic tile floors, PVC windows, drop ceilings |
| High-end engineered | $110-125+ | Imported finishes, backup power provisions, premium fixtures |

**Important**: NGO/humanitarian baselines ($14-$33/sq ft) lack rebar, MEP, and engineered connections. They are not valid comparisons for properly engineered construction. Do not use them as benchmarks.

### Cost Structure

- Materials: ~85% of total cost (inverted from developed-country norms where labor dominates)
- Labor: ~10-15% of total cost
- Rebar is the single largest material cost line item and the most dangerous place to cut corners

### Key Unit Costs

| Item | Approximate Cost | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-------|
| Portland cement | ~$8/bag (42.5 kg) | Price volatile. Buy known brands: Ciment d'Haiti, National, or imported. Store dry and elevated. |
| Concrete block (15cm) | ~$2/block | Quality varies widely. Inspect for consistent dimensions and curing. |
| Rebar | $580-$710/ton | Stock fluctuates. Order full quantity at once if possible. Confirm grade markings. |
| Mason daily wage | $6-$10/day | Skilled masons, varies by region and project |
| Laborer daily wage | $3-$6/day | Unskilled labor |
| Import tax on materials | 17-27% | Varies by material classification |

### Budget Planning Rules

- Always budget 15-20% contingency
- Confined masonry costs ~30% more than unreinforced masonry, justified by resilience ROI
- Fond-des-Blancs is 3-5 hours from Port-au-Prince; security conditions add 30-50%+ to base material transport costs
- PVC bronze-glass windows: ~$4K-$7.5K for a typical house, likely imported from Port-au-Prince, 4-8 week lead time
- Material transport surcharge to Fond-des-Blancs: ~$2K-$5K depending on volume
- Cement price volatility: $2/bag swing across 150-200 bags = $300-$400 budget impact

| Material | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Concrete block (15cm, 10cm) | Quality varies. Inspect for consistent dimensions and curing. |
| Sand and gravel | Must be clean -- no organic material, salt, or excess clay. River sand preferred over pit sand. |

## Resilience ROI References

These references are useful when justifying higher construction costs to donors, boards, or project funders:

- **IMF**: Resilient Caribbean infrastructure costs ~25% more than standard construction
- **World Bank**: $4 return per $1 invested in resilient building in developing countries
- **IDB**: Lifecycle analysis shows resilient construction is economically beneficial across wide scenarios over 50 years
- **Build Change**: Trained 7,000+ masons and 600 Haitian engineers since 2010; MTPTC adopted their retrofit guidelines as official standards in 2013
- **Habitat for Humanity**: TECLA houses survived the 2021 earthquake with zero casualties
- **"Recipe for Disaster" (Marshall et al.)**: Documents 2010 earthquake failures from under-reinforced CMU and poor concrete quality. Typical Haitian house: 3 #4 bars per column; properly engineered: 8 #6 bars (6x more steel)

## Concrete Quality (Critical)

- Target: 4,000 PSI for all structural elements
- Hand-mix field ratio: 1 part cement : 2 parts sand : 3 parts gravel (1:2:3 by volume)
- Water: 25 liters per 50kg cement bag for 4,000 PSI in hand-mixed conditions
- Water control is critical: too much water = weak concrete; too little = unworkable mix
- Most common failure mode: under-dosing cement (using too much sand/gravel per bag). Monitor every pour.
- Cure all poured elements wet for minimum 7 days (wet burlap, plastic sheeting, or regular water spray)
- In Haiti's heat, concrete that dries too fast never reaches full strength

## Seismic and Hurricane Context

- Haiti sits on the Enriquillo-Plantain Garden Fault zone
- Spectral acceleration: SDS = 1.05g-1.67g (highest seismic hazard zone)
- Design wind speed for southern Haiti: minimum 150 mph / 240 km/h
- Confined masonry (tied columns + bond beams + block infill) is the gold standard for this dual hazard
- The intermediate bond beam at mid-wall height is the single most important seismic feature after the foundation -- it prevents wall buckling during earthquakes. Never omit it.
