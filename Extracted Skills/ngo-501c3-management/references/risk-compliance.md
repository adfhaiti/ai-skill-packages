## Enterprise Risk Management

### Risk-Aware Framework (Herman, 2024)

Move beyond risk avoidance to risk awareness: understanding and managing risks
to advance mission rather than eliminating them. Nonprofits that take no risks
achieve no impact.

### ERM Process

1. Establish context: mission, strategic objectives, stakeholder expectations
2. Identify risks across all organizational domains
3. Analyze risks: likelihood × impact
4. Evaluate risks: prioritize for action
5. Treat risks: avoid, reduce, transfer (insurance), or accept
6. Monitor and review: ongoing risk assessment, not one-time exercise
7. Communicate and consult: engage stakeholders throughout

### Risk Categories

- **Strategic:** Risks to mission achievement, reputation, competitive position
- **Operational:** Program delivery, service quality, technology, supply chain
- **Financial:** Revenue volatility, investment risk, liquidity, fraud
- **Compliance:** Legal/regulatory violations, filing failures, grant noncompliance
- **Governance:** Board dysfunction, conflicts of interest, succession gaps
- **Human capital:** Key person dependency, workforce safety, misconduct

## Risk Identification & Assessment

### Risk Identification Methods

- Facilitated risk workshops with board, leadership, and program staff
- Environmental scanning: sector trends, regulatory changes, economic conditions
- Incident analysis: review of past problems, near misses, sector cases
- Stakeholder feedback: what do clients, funders, partners worry about?
- Insurance loss runs: what claims history reveals about exposure

### Risk Rating (Herman, 2024)

| Level | Likelihood | Impact | Action |
|-------|-----------|--------|--------|
| High | Probable (>50%) | Severe (threatens viability or mission) | Must mitigate; board-level oversight |
| Medium | Possible (10-50%) | Moderate (significant disruption) | Mitigation plan with monitoring |
| Low | Unlikely (<10%) | Minor (manageable within operations) | Accept or minimal controls |

Rate both inherent risk (before controls) and residual risk (after controls).
Focus mitigation resources where residual risk remains high.

### Risk Mitigation Planning

For each significant risk, document: risk description, risk owner (who is
accountable), existing controls, additional controls needed, implementation
timeline, resources required, and monitoring indicators.

## Insurance

### Essential Nonprofit Insurance

- **General liability:** Third-party bodily injury and property damage claims.
  Essential for any organization with facilities or public interaction.
- **Directors & Officers (D&O):** Defense and settlement of claims alleging
  wrongful acts in governance. Includes employment practices liability.
  Essential for any organization with a board.
- **Workers' compensation:** State-mandated coverage for employee injuries.
  Requirements vary by state.
- **Property:** Building and contents coverage. Business interruption option
  for loss of use.
- **Cyber liability:** Data breach response, notification costs, defense of
  privacy claims. Increasingly essential for any organization holding
  personal data.
- **Professional liability (Errors & Omissions):** Claims alleging negligent
  professional services (counseling, medical, legal services).
- **Fidelity bond/Crime:** Employee dishonesty and theft coverage.
- **Special event:** Coverage for short-duration events not covered by
  general liability.
- **Umbrella/Excess:** Additional limits above primary policies.

### Insurance Program Management

- Annual review of coverage adequacy with broker
- Competitive market review every 3-5 years
- Report claims promptly; early intervention reduces costs
- Understand exclusions and limitations, not just coverage grants
- Coordinate with risk management (insurance is risk transfer, not risk management)

## Internal Controls & Fraud Prevention

### Fraud Triangle

Three conditions present in most fraud cases: pressure (financial need, addiction,
lifestyle), opportunity (weak controls, trust without verification), and
rationalization ("I deserve it," "they won't miss it," "I'll pay it back").
Address all three: reduce pressures through fair compensation and supportive
culture, eliminate opportunities through strong controls, and counter
rationalization through ethical culture and accountability.

### Red Flags

- Employee reluctance to take vacation (fraud often requires continuous attention)
- Close relationships with vendors without documented competitive procurement
- Override of established controls by management
- Incomplete or missing documentation for transactions
- Lifestyle beyond apparent means
- Unexplained budget variances in areas under single-person control
- Defensive or evasive responses to auditor questions

### Fraud Response Plan

- Designate response team before incident occurs
- Secure evidence immediately upon discovery
- Engage legal counsel before investigation
- Consider insurance carrier notification requirements
- Prepare internal and external communication strategies
- Determine whether to involve law enforcement
- Address control failures that enabled the fraud

## Crisis Management

### Crisis Preparation

- Identify likely crisis scenarios based on organizational risk profile
- Designate crisis response team with clear roles
- Develop holding statements for immediate response
- Identify and train spokespersons
- Establish communication protocols and approval processes
- Pre-position relationships with legal counsel, PR support, insurance broker
- Test plan through tabletop exercises

### Crisis Response

1. **Assess:** Gather verified facts — what actually happened? Do not speculate.
2. **Activate team:** Convene crisis response team immediately.
3. **Contain:** Address immediate safety concerns, secure assets, preserve evidence.
4. **Communicate:** Internal stakeholders first (board, staff), then external
   (funders, partners, clients, media). Be first, be right, be credible.
5. **Manage:** Address operational continuity, legal exposure, reputational impact.
6. **Recover:** Return to normal operations, conduct after-action review, update
   plans based on lessons learned.

### Crisis Communication Principles

- Acknowledge the situation promptly — silence is interpreted as guilt or
  indifference
- Express genuine concern for those affected before addressing organizational
  implications
- Be transparent about what is known, unknown, and being done
- Never say "no comment" — explain why information cannot be shared
- Coordinate messaging — one voice, consistent across channels
- Monitor media and social media; correct misinformation promptly

## Ethical Management

### Core Values Framework (Jeavons, 2024)

Nonprofit ethics grounded in: integrity (honesty, consistency between words and
deeds), openness (transparency in governance and operations), accountability
(accepting responsibility for actions and outcomes), service (primacy of mission
and beneficiaries over organizational or personal interests), and charity
(responsible stewardship of resources entrusted by others).

### Ethical Decision-Making

1. Identify the ethical issue and affected stakeholders
2. Gather relevant facts and applicable policies/laws
3. Consider alternative courses of action
4. Evaluate alternatives against ethical principles and organizational values
5. Consult with trusted advisors (while respecting confidentiality)
6. Make decision and document reasoning
7. Implement, communicate, and monitor

### Creating Ethical Culture

- Leadership modeling: ethical behavior flows from the top
- Written Code of Ethics adopted by board and signed by all staff
- Regular ethics training and discussion, not just policy distribution
- Safe reporting mechanisms: whistleblower policy, ombuds, open door
- Accountability: ethics violations addressed consistently regardless of position
- Ethical dimensions integrated into decision-making at all levels

## Compliance Calendar

| Frequency | Filing/Action |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Monthly** | Payroll tax deposits (if applicable), financial statement review |
| **Quarterly** | Form 941 (employer's quarterly federal tax return), state unemployment tax, state withholding |
| **Semi-annual** | Review lobbying activity against 501(h) limits (if elected) |
| **Annual (calendar year)** | Form W-2s (Jan 31), Form 1099-NEC (Jan 31), Form 1099-MISC (Feb 28 / Mar 31 if e-file) |
| **Annual (fiscal year)** | Form 990 series (15th day of 5th month after FY close), state charitable solicitation renewal, Secretary of State annual report, property tax exemption renewal |
| **As needed** | Form 990-T (UBIT), Form 1023/1023-EZ (initial application), Form 8868 (extension), state-specific registrations |

### Regulatory Scanning

Monitor changes in: IRS regulations and guidance, state charitable solicitation laws,
employment law (federal, state, local), data privacy regulations (GDPR, CCPA, state
laws), and funder compliance requirements (Uniform Guidance for federal grants).
Assign responsibility for regulatory monitoring; do not rely on chance awareness.
