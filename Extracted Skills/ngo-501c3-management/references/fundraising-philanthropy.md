## Development Planning

### The Development Plan

A comprehensive, written plan covering 12-36 months that specifies: fundraising goals
by source, strategies and activities, timeline, responsible parties, budget, and
evaluation metrics. Annual development plan is a subset of a broader development
strategy.

### Revenue Pyramid

From broad base to narrow peak:
1. **Annual fund:** Many donors at small/moderate levels — broad base, acquisition focus
2. **Major gifts:** Fewer donors at significant levels — relationship cultivation,
   personal solicitation
3. **Planned giving:** Small number of legacy commitments — long-term cultivation,
   professional advisors
4. **Capital/endowment campaigns:** Largest commitments — board leadership,
   campaign structure

### Setting Fundraising Goals

Base goals on: strategic plan requirements, historical performance and trends, pipeline
analysis (prospective donors and anticipated amounts), organizational capacity (staff,
systems, board engagement), and external environment (economic conditions, competitive
landscape).

## Donor Cultivation Cycle

### Five-Phase Cycle

1. **Identification:** Research and identify prospective donors with capacity
   (financial ability), inclination (interest in cause), and linkage (connection
   to organization). Use wealth screening, peer referrals, event attendees,
   program participants, and current donors' networks.

2. **Qualification:** Determine whether the prospect has genuine interest and
   appropriate capacity. Initial outreach to gauge interest; "discovery visits"
   to learn about the prospect's philanthropic priorities.

3. **Cultivation:** Build relationship and deepen engagement before asking. Involves:
   educational events, program tours, volunteer opportunities, personal meetings,
   impact communications, and involvement of board/leadership volunteers.

4. **Solicitation:** Make the specific ask. Key principles: the right person asks
   (peer-to-peer solicitation is most effective), for the right project, for the
   right amount, at the right time, in the right way. Most solicitations should
   be face-to-face.

5. **Stewardship:** Thank, report, and engage after the gift. Prompt acknowledgment,
   meaningful impact reporting, continued involvement opportunities, and
   personalized communication. Stewardship is cultivation for the next gift.

### The Pipeline

Track all prospects through cultivation stages. Pipeline analysis: total prospect
value × probability by stage = expected value. A healthy pipeline has 3-4× the
annual goal in active prospects. Monitor pipeline velocity (movement through stages)
and conversion rates.

## Fundraising Methods

### Individual Giving

The largest source of U.S. philanthropy (~69% of total). Methods: direct mail,
personal solicitation, donor-advised funds, monthly giving programs, tribute/
memorial gifts, workplace giving, donor circles/giving societies.

### Major Gifts

Typically defined as top 10-20% of donors accounting for 80-90% of individual
contributions. Require: dedicated portfolio management (25-50 prospects per gift
officer), researched cultivation strategies, personalized proposals, and
relationship-based approach over transactional.

### Planned Giving

Deferred gifts through: bequests (most common), charitable remainder trusts,
charitable gift annuities, beneficiary designations (retirement accounts, life
insurance), and retained life estates. Requires specialized knowledge but can
generate transformational gifts. Marketing to all ages; closing often with
donors 65+.

### Capital Campaigns

Intensive, time-limited efforts to raise significant funds for a specific purpose
(building, endowment, major program expansion). Phases: planning/feasibility study,
quiet phase (60-70% of goal from lead gifts before public launch), public phase,
completion. Requires: strong board leadership, campaign cabinet, professional counsel,
feasibility study, and compelling case for support.

### Special Events

Generally inefficient for net revenue but valuable for: donor acquisition, cultivation,
visibility, and volunteer engagement. Target net revenue (after direct costs) of
50% or better. Best events align with mission and organizational identity.

## Grant Writing

### Foundation Research

Use Candid/Foundation Directory Online, state association of nonprofits, and peer
referrals to identify foundation prospects. Research: funding priorities, geographic
restrictions, average grant size, application process and deadlines, past grantees
(similar to your organization?), and decision-making process (staff-driven vs.
trustee-driven).

### Proposal Components

1. **Executive summary:** One-page overview — need, solution, organization credibility,
   funding request, expected outcomes
2. **Statement of need:** Evidence-based description of the problem; use data and
   community voice
3. **Project description:** Goals, objectives, activities, timeline, key personnel;
   logic model connecting activities to outcomes
4. **Organizational capacity:** History, mission alignment, past results, staff
   qualifications, unique assets
5. **Evaluation plan:** How outcomes will be measured, by whom, and when; evaluation
   use plan
6. **Sustainability:** How project continues after grant period
7. **Budget:** Detailed project budget with narrative; show other funding sources
8. **Attachments:** IRS determination letter, audited financials, board list, key
   staff bios

### Grant Management

Track: application deadlines, reporting requirements, grant restrictions, spending
against budget, match requirements, and allowability of costs. Timely and transparent
reporting — communicate problems early, not at report deadline.

## Donor Relations & Stewardship

### Acknowledgment Requirements

IRS requires written acknowledgment for gifts of $250 or more. Must include: amount
of cash or description of non-cash gift, statement of whether goods/services were
provided in exchange, and good-faith estimate of value of goods/services provided.
Quid pro quo contributions over $75 require additional disclosure.

### Stewardship Best Practices

- Acknowledge within 48 hours for all gifts
- Personalize based on donor relationship and gift significance
- Report impact, not just activities — "Your gift provided X to Y people, resulting in Z"
- Involve program staff and beneficiaries in stewardship communication
- Don't immediately ask again after a gift — balance stewardship and solicitation
- Treat donor data as a stewardship responsibility, not just a fundraising tool

### Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)

Growing vehicle. Donor receives tax deduction when contributing to DAF; sponsoring
organization makes grants to charities. DAF sponsors are public charities; notify
donors that contributions to your organization via DAF are complete (no further
tax deduction available).

## Corporate & Institutional Giving

### Corporate Engagement Spectrum

Transaction-based (sponsorship, event support) → partnership (multi-year, aligned
with corporate strategy) → integrated (shared value, skills-based volunteering,
cause marketing). Move relationships toward partnership over time.

### Sponsorship vs. Philanthropy

Sponsorship payments may be subject to UBIT if the sponsor receives substantial
return benefit (advertising, acknowledgment). Qualified sponsorship payments (mere
acknowledgment without qualitative language, logos without endorsement) are exempt.

## Digital & Online Fundraising

### Online Giving

Optimize: simple donation process (fewer clicks = higher conversion), mobile-first
design, recurring gift option prominent, suggested amounts with impact descriptions,
and immediate automated acknowledgment. Email remains highest ROI channel for most
nonprofits.

### Social Media Fundraising

Peer-to-peer fundraising campaigns, Facebook/Instagram fundraisers, Giving Tuesday,
and crowdfunding. Social proof drives giving — early donations beget more donations.
Platform fees vary; note that Facebook fundraisers disburse through Network for Good
(not directly to charity).

## Fundraising Ethics

### AFP Code of Ethical Standards

Key principles: donor privacy and confidentiality, truthful and accurate solicitation
communications, proper use of funds per donor intent, no commissions or contingent
compensation, transparency about fees and costs, and respect for donor autonomy.

### Donor Bill of Rights

Donors have the right: to be informed of the organization's mission, to know the
identity of board members, to access recent financial statements, to be assured
gifts will be used for intended purposes, to receive appropriate acknowledgment,
to confidentiality, and to professional behavior from fundraising staff.

## Servant-Leadership Fundraising

### Wildstein's Approach (2023)

Reframe fundraising from transactional solicitation to servant-leadership:

1. **Service over solicitation:** Fundraising is an invitation to participate in
   meaningful work, not a transaction
2. **Donor-centric discovery:** Understand donor values, motivations, and
   philanthropic goals before asking
3. **Abundance mindset:** There is enough money in the community; focus on
   aligning donor passions with organizational mission
4. **Gratitude as strategy:** Authentic, timely, and personal gratitude is the
   foundation of long-term donor relationships
5. **Story over statistics:** Stories move donors; statistics validate decisions
6. **Board as ambassadors:** Board members don't need to be "fundraisers" — they
   need to authentically share why they care

**Practical techniques:** gratitude visits (no ask), donor listening sessions,
impact storytelling workshops, board fundraising readiness assessment, and
shifting from "we need" to "you can make possible" language.
