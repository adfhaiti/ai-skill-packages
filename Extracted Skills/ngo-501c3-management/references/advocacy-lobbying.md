## Advocacy vs. Lobbying

### Advocacy (Unlimited for 501c3)

Advocacy is the broadest category — any activity to influence attitudes,
policies, or practices. Permissible without limit for 501(c)(3) organizations.
Includes: public education campaigns, community organizing, nonpartisan
research and analysis, litigation, regulatory comments, and educating
policymakers about issues without specific legislation reference.

### Lobbying (Limited for 501c3)

Lobbying is a subset of advocacy: attempting to influence specific legislation.
501(c)(3) organizations may lobby within limits. Two tests:

1. **Insubstantial part test (default):** Lobbying must not be a "substantial
   part" of activities. Vague, subjective, and risky — no clear numerical
   standard. Organizations without 501(h) election operate under this test.

2. **Expenditure test (501h election):** Clear, measurable limits based on
   organizational expenditures. Strongly recommended for any organization
   that lobbies more than incidentally.

### What is NOT Lobbying

- Discussing broad social issues without reference to specific legislation
- Responding to written requests from legislative bodies for technical advice
- Self-defense communications with legislators about matters affecting the
  organization's existence, powers, or tax-exempt status
- Nonpartisan analysis, study, or research presenting all sides of an issue
- Examinations of broad social, economic, and similar problems
- Regulatory advocacy (comments on proposed regulations from executive agencies)
- Litigation (seeking to enforce or clarify existing law)

## 501(h) Election

### Making the Election

File Form 5768 (one page, no fee). Takes effect immediately upon filing and
covers all subsequent years until revoked. Electing organizations report
lobbying expenditures on Schedule C of Form 990.

### Expenditure Limits

Based on "exempt purpose expenditures" (total expenditures excluding
fundraising, capital items, and unrelated business expenses):

| Exempt Purpose Expenditures | Total Lobbying Limit | Grassroots Lobbying Limit (25% of total) |
|----------------------------|---------------------|----------------------------------------|
| ≤ $500,000 | 20% | 5% |
| $500,001–$1,000,000 | $100,000 + 15% of excess over $500k | $25,000 + 3.75% of excess over $500k |
| $1,000,001–$1,500,000 | $175,000 + 10% of excess over $1M | $43,750 + 2.5% of excess over $1M |
| > $1,500,000 | $225,000 + 5% of excess over $1.5M | $56,250 + 1.25% of excess over $1.5M |

Maximum total lobbying: $1,000,000. Maximum grassroots lobbying: $250,000.

### Exceeding Limits

Exceeding the 501(h) limit by more than 50% over a 4-year averaging period
(typically 150% of limit in any one year) results in loss of 501(c)(3) status.
Lesser excesses incur a 25% excise tax on the excess amount.

### Recordkeeping Under 501(h)

Track: all lobbying expenditures (staff time, communications, travel, overhead
allocation), how amounts were allocated between direct and grassroots lobbying,
and activities counted vs. excluded under exceptions. Maintain documentation
for at least 3 years after filing.

## Direct vs. Grassroots Lobbying

### Direct Lobbying

Communication with a legislator (or legislative staff) or government official
who participates in formulating legislation that: refers to specific legislation,
and reflects a view on such legislation.

Includes: communication with the general public about a specific legislative
proposal that asks the public to contact their legislators (it's the "call to
action" that makes it direct lobbying expenditure, not grassroots).

### Grassroots Lobbying

Communication with the general public that: refers to specific legislation,
reflects a view on such legislation, AND includes a "call to action" (asks
the public to contact legislators, provides legislator contact information,
or identifies legislators' positions).

Without a call to action, communication to the public about legislation is
advocacy, not lobbying.

### Allocation Rules

- If communication has both lobbying and non-lobbying purposes, allocate
  costs based on relative purposes (e.g., by space or time)
- Membership communications: if lobbying content exceeds non-lobbying content,
  must allocate to lobbying
- Paid mass media: advertising that meets lobbying definition is lobbying
  expenditure for the full cost

## Political Campaign Intervention Prohibition

### Absolute Prohibition

501(c)(3) organizations are absolutely prohibited from participating in or
intervening in any political campaign on behalf of or in opposition to any
candidate for public office. This includes: endorsements, contributions,
statements of support or opposition, providing resources or facilities to
candidates on a preferential basis, and voter guides that compare candidates
on a narrow range of issues in a way that implies endorsement.

### What IS Permitted

- Nonpartisan voter registration and get-out-the-vote drives
- Nonpartisan voter education (candidate forums where all candidates are invited,
  broad issue questionnaires)
- Issue advocacy that doesn't reference candidates or elections
- Individual political activity by staff and board members in personal capacity
  (must not use organizational resources or imply organizational endorsement)
- Candidate appearances in non-candidate capacity (e.g., as an expert speaking
  on issues, not as a candidate)

### Social Media Caution

Blogs, social media posts, and organizational websites are organizational
communications subject to the political campaign intervention prohibition.
Likes, shares, and retweets can create risks. Establish a social media policy
that addresses political activity during election seasons.

## Advocacy Strategy

### Strategy Development (Mason, 2024)

1. **Define the advocacy goal:** Specific, achievable policy change or systems
   shift. Distinguish between long-term vision and campaign goal.
2. **Analyze the policy landscape:** Who decides? What influences them? What
   is the political context? Where is the leverage?
3. **Identify target audiences:** Primary (decision-makers), secondary
   (influencers of decision-makers), and tertiary (constituency whose support
   is needed)
4. **Develop messages:** Frame issue in terms of target audience values;
   clear problem, solution, and ask; tested with intended audience
5. **Select tactics:** Lobbying, grassroots mobilization, media advocacy,
   coalition building, research and policy analysis, litigation
6. **Build capacity:** Organizational readiness, coalition infrastructure,
   communications capacity, fundraising for advocacy
7. **Implement and adapt:** Monitor political environment, adjust tactics
   as windows of opportunity open and close

### Power Mapping

Visual tool for identifying influence relationships. Map: who has power to
make the decision, who influences those decision-makers, what is the
organization's relationship to each, and what leverage the organization
has with each. Use to prioritize engagement and identify coalition partners.

### Coalition Building

Advocacy coalitions amplify voice and share risk. Key considerations: shared
policy agenda, decision-making processes (consensus vs. majority with opt-out),
resource commitments, messaging coordination, media strategy (who speaks for
the coalition), and sustainability beyond a single campaign.

## Measuring Advocacy Impact

### Beyond Policy Wins

Policy change (bill passed, regulation adopted) is the most visible outcome,
but advocacy also produces: stronger coalitions, increased public awareness,
shifted public discourse, engaged constituencies with advocacy skills,
established organizational credibility with policymakers, and improved
implementation of existing policies.

### Advocacy Evaluation Framework

- **Inputs:** Resources invested in advocacy (staff, funds, volunteer time)
- **Activities:** Actions taken (meetings held, media hits, coalition members
  engaged)
- **Outputs:** Direct products (policy briefs published, petition signatures,
  legislator contacts)
- **Outcomes:** Changes resulting (policy adopted, funding increased,
  implementation improved, public opinion shifted)
- **Impact:** Ultimate changes in conditions for affected populations

### Advocacy Capacity Assessment

Assess: clear policy agenda linked to mission, dedicated advocacy staff or
function, board support for advocacy, legal compliance systems, coalition
relationships, communications capacity, funding for advocacy (including
non-lobbying advocacy that has no legal limits), and ability to operate
in political environment.

## Nonpartisan Voter Engagement

### Permissible Activities

501(c)(3) organizations may: conduct nonpartisan voter registration drives,
provide nonpartisan voter education, encourage voting generally, provide
transportation to polls (if provided without regard to party), and host
candidate forums (if all candidates invited, broad range of issues, no
implied endorsement).

### 501(c)(4) Affiliate Option

Organizations that want to engage in significant lobbying or limited political
activity may establish a 501(c)(4) social welfare organization as an affiliate.
Contributions to 501(c)(4)s are not tax-deductible. The 501(c)(3) and (c)(4)
must maintain separate finances, governance, and operations to avoid
attribution. Shared staff and facilities must be allocated at fair market value.
