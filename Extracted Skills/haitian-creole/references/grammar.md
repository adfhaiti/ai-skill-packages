# Haitian Creole Grammar Reference

## Sentence Structure

HC follows Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order, the same as English. However, several structural differences matter for translation:

### Word Order in Noun Phrases

Adjectives follow the noun (like French, unlike English):
- yon gwo kay = a big house
- yon bèl fi = a beautiful girl/woman
- dlo frèt = cold water

But some high-frequency adjectives can precede: gwo (big), ti/piti (small), bon (good), move (bad).

### Demonstratives

"Sa a" (this/that) follows the noun phrase:
- liv sa a = this/that book
- moun sa yo = these/those people

### Topic-Comment Structure

HC frequently uses topic-comment constructions that English does not:
- "Liv la, mwen li l" = (As for) the book, I read it
- This is natural HC, not a grammatical error

## Verb System Deep Dive

### Bare Verb (Zero Marker)

With no TMA marker, the verb expresses:
- Habitual present: "Li manje diri chak jou" (He eats rice every day)
- General truth: "Solèy la leve nan lès" (The sun rises in the east)
- Immediate/narrative present: "Li rive, li chita, li kòmanse pale" (He arrives, sits down, starts talking)

### Serial Verb Constructions

HC uses serial verbs (two or more verbs in sequence without conjunction) more extensively than English:
- "Li kouri ale" = He ran away (kouri + ale)
- "Li pran liv la bay mwen" = He took the book and gave it to me (pran + bay)
- "Vin chita" = Come sit down (vin + chita)

### Copula

HC uses several copular constructions (Fedexy 2015, Section 22):
- "se" for identification/equation: "Li se doktè" (He is a doctor)
- "ye" for wh-questions: "Ki sa li ye?" (What is it?)
- Zero copula for adjective predication: "Li malad" (He is sick), "Kay la gwo" (The house is big)
- Obligatory copula: when the predicate is a noun phrase identifying the subject
- Optional copula: varies by dialect and register
- Impossible copula: cannot use copula with adjective predicates (*Li se malad is ungrammatical)

### Imperative

No special form; context and intonation indicate commands:
- "Vini!" (Come!)
- "Pa fè sa!" (Don't do that!)
- "Ann ale!" / "Annou ale!" (Let's go!)

Note: "Annou" is a single word (imperative first-person plural marker). Both the AKA modifier test and Zefi's commutation test confirm this: you cannot insert anything between "an" and "nou" (*An ou ale! is ungrammatical).

## Nine Morphosyntactic Categories (Fedexy 2015)

Fedexy identifies nine categories in two groups:

**Open categories (kategori mo leksik):** Non (noun), Adjektif, Veb, Adveb
**Closed categories (kategori mo grame):** Deteminan, Pwonon, Prepozisyon, Konjonksyon, Mak tan ak aspe (TMA markers)

TMA markers are their own category, not verbs or auxiliaries. They function in association with the verb but lack autonomous syntactic behavior.

## The Definite Article System (Detailed)

### Phonological Conditioning

The definite article form is determined by the final sound of the **word immediately preceding the article** (Lefebvre/Fournier 1979; Fedexy 2015). If an adjective intervenes between noun and article, the adjective's final sound governs the form:

| Form | Phonological context | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| a | after oral vowel | papa a, bebe a, lari a, lakou a |
| an | after nasal vowel | ban an, pen an, bonbon an |
| la | after oral consonant preceded by oral vowel | bab la, nich la, rad la, kok la, lwil la, liv la |
| lan | after oral consonant preceded by nasal vowel | tanp lan, tenb lan, chif onz lan |
| nan | after nasal consonant | pom nan, kizin nan, paking nan |

Plural: "yo" (invariable). No phonological conditioning.

### The Two Homonymous "la" Particles

Lefebvre and Fournier (1979) demonstrate that HC has two distinct particles both spelled "la":

1. **la** as determiner (DET): postposed to the NP, undergoes phonological reduction (la → a, an, lan, nan)
2. **la** as adverb (ADV): meaning "there/here" (related to isit, isit la), does NOT undergo the same reductions

The combination "la la" in surface speech is always: DET la + ADV la (not an "emphatic" determiner). These are two separate morphemes that happen to be homophonous.

### Pragmatic Conditions for DET "la"

A noun takes the definite article only when the referent is "specified" through one of three conditions (Lefebvre/Fournier 1979):

1. **Previously mentioned in discourse**: first mention uses no DET; subsequent mentions take "la"
2. **Situationally present**: the referent is physically present and can be pointed to
3. **Shared cultural/experiential knowledge**: the referent is identifiable through shared experience

If none of these conditions is met, a noun with "la" is uninterpretable.

### Sentential "la"

"La" can mark entire clauses (not just nouns) to signal the proposition is presupposed/shared knowledge:
- "Zan te vini a" = Jean came (confirming what you already knew)
- "Zan te vini" = Jean came (new information)

This appears in: declaratives, indirect interrogatives, indirect imperatives, relative clauses, completives, cleft constructions, temporal/locative/manner/conditional adverbials. It does NOT appear in direct imperatives (*Vini a! is ungrammatical).

### Determiner Sharing

When a noun complement is a subordinate clause, the determiner can serve simultaneously for both the main noun and a noun within the clause: "Pran mamit ki sou tab la pou mwen" (la serves both mamit and tab).

## Possessive Determiner Paradigm (Fedexy 2015)

| Simple | Reinforced (pa) |
|---|---|
| kay mwen / kay m | kay pa m |
| kay ou / kay w | kay pa w |
| kay li / kay l | kay pa l |
| kay nou / kay n | kay pa n |
| kay yo | kay pa yo |

Possessive determiners are actually pronoun + definite determiner fused: mwen an / m nan, ou a / w la, li a / l la, nou an / n nan, yo a.

## Relative Clauses

"Ki" introduces relative clauses:
- "Moun ki rive a" = The person who arrived
- "Liv ki sou tab la" = The book that is on the table
- "Kote mwen te ye a" = (The place) where I was

## Reflexive and Reciprocal

- Reflexive: "tèt" + possessive pronoun: "Li lave tèt li" (He washed himself, lit: he washed his head)
- Reciprocal: "youn...lòt": "Yo renmen youn lòt" (They love each other)

## Comparison

- Comparative: "pi...pase": "Li pi gwo pase m" (He is bigger than me)
- Superlative: "pi...nan" or context: "Li se pi gwo a" (He is the biggest one)
- Equality: "menm jan ak": "Li gwo menm jan ak mwen" (He is as big as me)

## Focus and Emphasis

- "Se...ki" for cleft constructions: "Se li ki fè sa" (It is he who did that)
- Fronting for emphasis: "Diri m vle" (It's rice that I want)
- "menm" for emphasis: "Li menm" (he himself), "sa menm" (that very thing)
- Repetition for emphasis
- "Se pa" negation constructions
- "Ala/Ala de" exclamatives (using Fedexy's "deteminan santiman ak emosyon")

## Subordination System (Fedexy 2015)

Five types:
1. **Sibodone relatif** (relative subordinate clauses)
2. **Sibodone konpletif** (completive: indirect questions, sentiment/emotion clauses)
3. **Sibodone konpleman fraz** (clausal complements: time, cause, consequence, objective, condition, opposition, comparison, concession)
4. **Sibodone korelatif** (correlative subordinates)
5. **Ensesyon** (insertion/parenthetical clauses)

## Decreolization Awareness

Five categories of decreolization ("depèdisyon") in HC syntax (AKA "100 Kesyon" K99, from Chery 2023):
1. Word order changes
2. Passive voice adoption
3. Preposition simplification
4. Subjectless verbs
5. Indirect transitive replacing direct transitive

Example: "Naje pou sòti" (standard) vs. "Naje pou nou sòti" (decreolized). Watch for these patterns and prefer the standard HC construction in translation.
