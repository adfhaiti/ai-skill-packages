## Nonprofit Accounting Principles

### Fund Accounting

Nonprofits use fund accounting to track resources by purpose and restriction:

- **Unrestricted net assets:** Available for general operations; board-designated reserves
  fall here
- **Temporarily restricted net assets:** Donor-restricted for specific purpose or time
  period; released when conditions are met
- **Permanently restricted net assets:** Endowment principal; only investment earnings
  available for use (subject to donor restrictions and state law)

### FASB Standards

- **ASC 958 (formerly FAS 116/117):** Governs nonprofit financial reporting
- **Revenue recognition (ASC 606):** Exchange transactions recognized when performance
  obligations are satisfied
- **Contribution recognition (ASC 958-605):** Conditional contributions recognized when
  conditions are substantially met; unconditional contributions recognized when received
  or pledged (with allowance for uncollectible pledges)
- **Functional expense allocation:** Expenses must be reported by both natural
  classification (salaries, rent, supplies) and functional classification (program
  services, management & general, fundraising)

### Chart of Accounts

Design to support both financial reporting (GAAP/FASB) and management reporting
(budget vs. actual by program, grant tracking). Segregate: program expenses by
program area, management & general, fundraising. Use granular enough accounts
to track restricted grant spending.

## Financial Statements

### Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet)

Reports: assets (current, fixed, other), liabilities (current, long-term), and net assets
(without donor restrictions, with donor restrictions). Key insight: liquidity and financial
structure at a point in time.

### Statement of Activities (Income Statement)

Reports: revenues by source, expenses by functional classification, changes in net assets.
Key insight: sustainability of operations over a period, relationship between revenue
sources and expenses, changes in restricted vs. unrestricted resources.

### Statement of Cash Flows

Reports: cash flows from operating activities, investing activities, and financing
activities. Key insight: ability to generate cash to meet obligations. Many nonprofits
are profitable on accrual basis but face cash flow crises.

### Statement of Functional Expenses

Reports expenses by both natural and functional classification. Required for all voluntary
health and welfare organizations; others must present this information in notes. Key
insight: what percentage of resources goes to programs vs. administration and fundraising.

## Budgeting

### Budget Types

- **Line-item budget:** Expenses by object class (salaries, benefits, rent, supplies).
  Simple but doesn't connect spending to programs or goals.
- **Program budget:** Expenses allocated to specific programs plus management & general
  and fundraising. Connects spending to mission.
- **Performance budget:** Links program budgets to expected outputs and outcomes.
  Most informative but hardest to implement.
- **Flexible budget:** Adjusts for volume changes; especially useful for organizations
  with variable program volumes.
- **Cash budget:** Projects cash inflows and outflows by month/quarter to identify
  liquidity gaps.

### Budget Development Process

1. Establish budget calendar and guidelines
2. Develop program plans and associated costs
3. Project revenues from all sources (conservative estimates)
4. Balance: if expenses exceed realistic revenues, prioritize and cut
5. Board finance committee review
6. Full board approval
7. Monitor monthly with variance analysis

### Budget Monitoring & Variance Analysis

Monthly comparison of actual results to budget with variance explanations. Investigate
variances exceeding threshold (typically 5-10% or materially significant). Categories:
favorable/unfavorable, temporary/permanent, controllable/uncontrollable. Timely corrective
action for significant negative variances.

## Internal Controls

### Segregation of Duties

No single person should control all aspects of a financial transaction. Separate:
authorization, custody, record-keeping, and reconciliation. In small nonprofits with
limited staff: compensating controls (board review of bank statements, surprise audits,
dual signatures above threshold).

### Key Controls

- **Cash receipts:** Two-person opening of mail, immediate restrictive endorsement,
  log of receipts, timely deposit, reconciliation to donor database
- **Cash disbursements:** Purchase authorization, supporting documentation review,
  check signing authority (dual signatures above threshold), positive pay
- **Payroll:** Time sheet approval, independent review of payroll register,
  segregation from HR functions
- **Bank reconciliation:** Performed by someone without check signing or deposit
  authority, reviewed by ED or Finance Committee
- **Credit cards:** Receipts required for all charges, documented business purpose,
  reviewed by supervisor
- **Grants management:** Tracking restricted vs. unrestricted spending, time and
  effort reporting, compliance with grant terms

### Fraud Prevention

Nonprofits are vulnerable to fraud due to: trust-based culture, limited staffing,
reliance on volunteers, and funder focus on program ratios. Red flags: reluctance to
take vacation, lifestyle beyond means, close relationships with vendors, override of
controls, inadequate documentation.

## Financial Ratio Analysis

### Liquidity Ratios

- **Current ratio:** Current assets / Current liabilities. Target > 2.0.
- **Quick ratio:** (Cash + Receivables) / Current liabilities. Target > 1.0.
- **Days of cash on hand:** (Cash + Cash equivalents) / (Monthly operating expenses −
  non-cash expenses). Target: 3-6 months.

### Leverage Ratios

- **Debt to net assets:** Total liabilities / Total net assets. No universal target;
  lower generally better for nonprofits.
- **Debt service coverage:** (Change in net assets + Depreciation + Interest) /
  (Principal + Interest payments). Target > 1.2.

### Efficiency Ratios

- **Program expense ratio:** Program expenses / Total expenses. 65-75% generally
  considered reasonable; varies by subsector.
- **Fundraising efficiency:** Fundraising expenses / Total contributions. Under 35%
  typical; varies by organization age and fundraising strategy.
- **Management & general ratio:** M&G expenses / Total expenses. 10-20% typical.

### Sustainability Ratios

- **Operating margin:** (Operating revenue − Operating expenses) / Operating revenue.
  Target: breakeven to 3-5% surplus.
- **Revenue concentration:** Largest revenue source / Total revenue. No single source
  should exceed 25-30% without diversification strategy.
- **Personnel costs:** Personnel expenses / Total expenses. Varies widely by subsector
  (50-80% typical).

## Revenue Diversification

### Typical Revenue Sources (Young & Searing, 2024)

Revenue mix varies dramatically by subsector: arts organizations rely heavily on earned
revenue; human services depend on government contracts; religious organizations on
individual contributions. Average mix: individual giving ~69% of all giving, foundations
~19%, bequests ~9%, corporations ~4%.

### Diversification Strategy

- **Too concentrated:** One funder >30% of revenue creates existential risk
- **Healthy diversification:** Mix of individual donors, foundation grants, government
  contracts, earned revenue, special events, and investment income
- **Diversification cost:** Each revenue source requires different skills, systems,
  and relationship management; balance diversification benefits against complexity costs
- **Cash vs. non-cash revenue:** In-kind contributions are revenue but not cash;
  depreciation is an expense but not cash — manage cash separately from accrual

### Earned Income Models

- Fee-for-service: direct payment for services delivered (sliding scale or market rate)
- Social enterprise: business operation that advances mission
- Membership dues: payment for affiliation benefits
- Licensing and royalties: monetization of intellectual property
- Facility rental: utilization of space assets
- Contract services: providing services to government or other organizations

## Cash Flow & Reserves

### Cash Flow Management

Project cash flows 12 months forward, updated monthly. Identify seasonal patterns:
many nonprofits receive peak donations in Q4 but have steady expenses year-round.
Manage: accelerate receivables (pledge reminders, grant billing), time payables
strategically, maintain line of credit for bridging seasonal gaps.

### Operating Reserves

Formal reserve policy defining: target level (typically 3-6 months of operating
expenses), conditions for use (not for budget shortfalls from structural deficits),
replenishment plan, and governance (board approval required to draw below target).

### Working Capital

Maintain adequate working capital (current assets minus current liabilities).
Growing organizations often face working capital challenges as program expansion
outpaces cash receipts.

## Audits

### When Required

- Federal awards exceeding $750,000: Single Audit (Uniform Guidance audit)
- State requirements vary: many require audit if revenues exceed threshold
  ($500,000-$2,000,000 common)
- Funder requirements: many foundation and government grants require audited
  financial statements
- Board policy: best practice to obtain audit or review even when not required

### Audit Preparation

- Reconcile all accounts before auditor arrival
- Prepare schedules: fixed assets and depreciation, grants receivable, pledges
  receivable with allowance, functional expense allocation
- Document: related party transactions, subsequent events, going concern considerations
- Draft financial statements and footnotes
- Prepare management representation letter

### Audit Committee

Independent directors (with financial literacy) oversee: auditor selection and
retention, review of audit scope and results, internal control findings, management
letter, and audited financial statements. Meet with auditors without management present.

## Dual Bottom Line Matrix

### Zimmerman's Framework

Nonprofits must balance mission impact (the nonprofit imperative) and financial
viability (the going-concern imperative). These create four quadrants:

| | Financially Sustainable | Financially Unsustainable |
|---|---|---|
| **High Impact** | Star — invest, grow | Heart — restructure to sustainability |
| **Low Impact** | Money — redirect to mission | Dog — divest or terminate |

Strategic decision-making should move programs toward the Star quadrant. Heart programs
need restructuring, not abandonment. Money programs need mission realignment. Dog
programs consume resources that could serve mission more effectively elsewhere.

### Application

Apply Dual Bottom Line analysis to each major program annually. Integrate with strategic
planning and budgeting. Use program evaluation data for mission impact assessment. Use
financial analysis for sustainability assessment. Board discussion should address the
full portfolio, not individual programs in isolation.
