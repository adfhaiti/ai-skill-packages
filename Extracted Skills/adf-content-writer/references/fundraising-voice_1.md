# ADF Haiti: Fundraising Voice Guide

## Core Framing

ADF's fundraising voice inverts standard nonprofit charity language. Donors are investors in local institutional capacity, not rescuers of a suffering population.

### Signature Contrasts (use in headlines, CTAs, and framing)

- "Fund systems, not symptoms"
- "Dignity, not dependency"
- "Respected, not rescued"
- Success measured "not by aid delivered, but by aid no longer required"
- Investment framing over charity framing

### Cause-and-Effect Structure

Every donor communication uses a simple three-beat structure:
1. **Problem:** A specific, concrete need (not a generic "Haiti is poor" framing)
2. **Gift:** What the donor's contribution enables (specific and tangible)
3. **Outcome:** The measurable result

Example pattern: "Right now, [specific situation]. Your [dollar amount] [specific action]. The result: [concrete outcome]."

## Donor Email Rules

- **Two accomplishments per email, maximum.** Not comprehensive reporting.
- **Personal CEO-authored voice** (Josiah Thomas). First person. "I" not "we" when appropriate.
- **Direct, authentic language.** Write like you're updating a friend who invested in your startup.
- **No formal report-style structure.** No headers, no bullet-heavy layouts. Just a person talking to another person.
- **No exhaustive updates.** Brief, focused, with a clear ask or clear thank-you.
- **Close with a specific ask** when fundraising. Not "please consider donating" but "Your $75 covers one enumerator's field supplies for a week of data collection."

## Thank-You Language

Approved: "We are grateful, and we thank you for embracing our cause. Our battle for justice and equality is made possible thanks to your generous support."

This can be adapted, but preserve the tone: grateful without groveling, assertive about the mission ("battle for justice"), and acknowledging the donor as a partner.

## What Never Appears in Donor Content

- Poverty imagery or descriptions designed to evoke pity
- Savior narratives ("we're saving Haiti" or "you can save a life")
- "Thriving," "prosperous," or other generic nonprofit cliches used as filler
- Defensive qualifiers ("despite the challenges," "in spite of Haiti's difficulties")
- Flowery or metaphorical language
- Exhaustive single-email updates
- Formal report-style structure in emails

## Appeal Letter Pattern (from 2019 Appeal)

The 2019 Year End Appeal used this structure:
1. Open with a specific, tangible scene from the field
2. One paragraph establishing the problem (local, specific, not "all of Haiti")
3. What ADF did about it (concrete actions, named projects)
4. The gap that remains (honest, specific)
5. What the donor's gift enables (tied to a specific project or deliverable)
6. Close with personal voice and signature
