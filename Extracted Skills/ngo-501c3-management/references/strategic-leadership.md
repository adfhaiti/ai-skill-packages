## Executive Leadership Competencies

### Dann's Four Practice Perspectives (Dann, 2022)

1. **Being Antigravitational:** Resist forces pulling the organization toward the status
   quo. Actively question assumptions, push boundaries, and create space for innovation.
   Recognize that gravitational pull comes from funders, board members, staff comfort,
   and sector norms.

2. **The Long View:** Maintain focus on long-term mission while managing immediate
   demands. Invest in capacity even when program demands are urgent. Develop multi-year
   strategies rather than annual plans.

3. **It's the Journey, Not the Destination:** Embrace process as product. Organizational
   development is ongoing — there is no endpoint. Build adaptive capacity rather than
   seeking permanent solutions.

4. **Leverage Serendipity:** Recognize and capitalize on unexpected opportunities.
   Maintain organizational readiness to pivot. Build relationships that create
   serendipitous connections.

### Key Executive Competencies (Carlson & Donohoe, 2010)

- **Visioning:** Articulating a compelling future and aligning stakeholders around it
- **Relationship building:** Cultivating relationships with board, staff, funders,
  community partners, and policymakers
- **Financial acumen:** Understanding nonprofit finance, budgeting, revenue strategy,
  and financial sustainability
- **Program knowledge:** Deep understanding of the mission domain and programmatic work
- **Self-management:** Emotional intelligence, resilience, work-life integration,
  personal renewal
- **Change management:** Leading organizational transformation while maintaining trust

### Joan Garry's Key Principles (Garry, 2020)

- Nonprofit leaders are often promoted from program roles without management training —
  seek out and develop management skills deliberately
- The board-ED relationship is the single most important organizational relationship;
  invest in it
- Strategy without execution is delusion; execution without strategy is chaos
- Fundraising is leadership, not begging — reframe the narrative
- Crisis reveals character; prepare for crises before they arrive

## Strategic Planning

### Strategy Change Cycle (Bryson & George, 2024)

A 10-step iterative process:

1. Initiate and agree on strategic planning process
2. Identify organizational mandates (legal, funder, stakeholder requirements)
3. Clarify mission and values
4. Assess external environment (SWOT/PESTEL analysis)
5. Assess internal environment (resources, capabilities, culture)
6. Identify strategic issues facing the organization
7. Formulate strategies to manage issues
8. Review and adopt the strategic plan
9. Establish an effective organizational vision
10. Develop effective implementation process

### Strategic Management Framework (Brown, 2024)

Nonprofit strategic management differs from for-profit because nonprofits serve multiple
stakeholders with diverse — often conflicting — expectations. Key considerations:

- **Multiple bottom lines:** Financial viability, mission impact, stakeholder legitimacy
- **Resource attraction vs. resource allocation:** Strategy must address both resource
  acquisition (fundraising, grants, earned revenue) and resource deployment (program
  delivery, capacity building)
- **Competitive and collaborative dynamics:** Strategy in a sector where organizations
  both compete and collaborate
- **Strategic inputs emerge from multiple markets:** Donor markets, client markets,
  volunteer markets, advocacy arenas

### Implementation

Strategic plans fail more often in implementation than in design. Critical success
factors: assign clear accountability, translate strategy into annual operating plans
and budgets, establish monitoring systems with leading indicators, schedule regular
strategy reviews, and maintain flexibility to adapt.

## Organizational Effectiveness Frameworks

### Multidimensional Integrated Model (Sowa, Selden, & Sandfort, 2004)

Effectiveness is best understood as multidimensional, requiring both management
effectiveness and program effectiveness:

- **Management effectiveness:** Organizational capacity — financial management,
  human resources, governance, information systems, external relations
- **Program effectiveness:** Program outcomes — outputs, quality, outcomes,
  client satisfaction
- Each dimension has objective and perceptual components
- Effectiveness is relative to the organization's stage of development

### Multiple Constituencies Approach (Herman & Renz, 1997, 1999)

Key theses on nonprofit effectiveness:

1. Nonprofit organizational effectiveness is socially constructed — different stakeholders
   define effectiveness differently
2. Boards are responsible for determining which effectiveness criteria to prioritize,
   but the criteria ultimately adopted are negotiated among stakeholders
3. Effective nonprofits are more likely to use correct management practices, but there
   is no universal set of "best practices"
4. Successful nonprofits use multiple indicators of effectiveness rather than relying
   on single measures
5. Responsiveness to changing stakeholder expectations is itself a form of effectiveness

### Best Practices Analysis (Liket & Maas, 2015)

Practices that align academic research with practitioner views:

- Clear mission and strategic direction
- Strong governance and board engagement
- Professional management and staff development
- Outcome measurement and program evaluation
- Financial transparency and sustainability
- Stakeholder engagement and communication
- Adaptive capacity and continuous improvement

## Mission & Strategy

### Mission as Strategic Anchor

The mission statement should define: who the organization serves, what need it addresses,
how it addresses it, and what makes its approach distinctive. Test every strategic decision
against mission alignment: "Does this advance our mission, or expand our scope beyond it?"

### Mission Drift

The gradual shift away from founding mission toward activities that are fundable rather
than mission-critical. Common drivers: chasing grant dollars, board or funder pressure,
opportunistic growth. Prevention: explicit mission boundaries in strategic plan, board
discipline in evaluating opportunities, regular mission alignment audits.

### Theory of Change

A comprehensive description of how and why a desired change is expected to happen in a
particular context. Articulates: the problem, the intervention, the mechanism of change
(causal pathway), assumptions, and measurable indicators.

**Logic model vs. Theory of Change:** A logic model depicts the program (inputs →
activities → outputs → outcomes → impact). A theory of change explains why the
activities are expected to lead to the outcomes.

## Social Enterprise & Hybrid Models

### Social Entrepreneurship

Applying entrepreneurial approaches to social problems. Key characteristics (Andersson
& Kerlin, 2024): innovativeness, proactiveness, risk-taking, social mission primacy.

### Social Enterprise Spectrum

Organizations range from purely philanthropic to purely commercial, with hybrid models
in between:

- **Mission-integrated:** Earned revenue directly advances mission (e.g., job training
  program that operates a business employing trainees)
- **Mission-related:** Earned revenue supports but does not directly achieve mission
  (e.g., museum gift shop)
- **Unrelated:** Earned revenue is separate from mission (subject to UBIT)

### Decision Framework (Faulk & Hall, 2024)

When evaluating new ventures or programs, assess: mission alignment, market demand,
organizational capacity, financial viability (startup costs, ongoing sustainability,
risk), competitive landscape, fit with organizational identity.

## Succession & Leadership Pipeline

### Types of Succession

- **Planned succession:** Orderly transition with overlap, knowledge transfer, and
  managed communication
- **Emergency succession:** Unexpected departure requiring interim leadership; board
  must have emergency plan in place
- **Founder succession:** Particularly challenging due to founder's institutional
  knowledge, donor relationships, and organizational identity tied to founder

### Succession Planning Process

1. Board initiates and governs the process
2. Assess organizational needs and strategic direction
3. Develop transition timeline and communication plan
4. Appoint transition committee
5. Determine interim leadership arrangement
6. Conduct search (internal candidates, external search, or hybrid)
7. Onboard new ED with intentional transition period
8. Support exiting ED transition (especially for founders/long-tenured leaders)

## Organizational Culture

### High-Performance Nonprofit Culture (Green & Hauser, 2012)

- **Results orientation:** Clear expectations, accountability for outcomes, celebration
  of achievement
- **Learning orientation:** Psychological safety, willingness to admit mistakes,
  continuous improvement
- **Mission obsession:** Mission drives decisions at all levels; staff connect daily
  work to mission impact
- **Collaborative practice:** Cross-functional teams, knowledge sharing, collective
  problem-solving
- **Equity and inclusion:** Diverse perspectives valued, equitable practices embedded

### Culture as Leadership Tool

Leaders shape culture through: what they pay attention to and measure, how they react
to crises, how they allocate resources, role modeling, how they hire and fire, and the
stories they tell. Culture eats strategy for breakfast — invest in it deliberately.
