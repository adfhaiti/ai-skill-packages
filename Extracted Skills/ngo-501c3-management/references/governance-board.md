## Fiduciary Duties

### Duty of Care

Act in good faith, with the care of an ordinarily prudent person, and in a manner
reasonably believed to be in the best interests of the organization. Requires:

- Regular meeting attendance and preparation
- Informed decision-making based on adequate information
- Reliance on experts when appropriate
- Diligent oversight of organizational activities

**Business judgment rule:** Directors who act on an informed basis, in good faith, and
without conflicts of interest are generally protected from liability for their decisions.

### Duty of Loyalty

Act in the interest of the organization, not personal interest. Requires:

- Full disclosure of conflicts of interest
- Abstention from decisions where a conflict exists
- No usurpation of corporate opportunities
- Maintaining confidentiality of board discussions

### Duty of Obedience

Ensure the organization remains faithful to its mission and complies with applicable
laws. Requires:

- Adherence to the organization's stated mission and purposes
- Compliance with articles of incorporation, bylaws, and policies
- Oversight of legal and regulatory compliance
- Ensuring funds are used for stated charitable purposes

## Board Composition & Structure

### Size

IRS recommends minimum of 3 independent directors. Optimal size: 7-15 members. Smaller
than 7 limits diversity of perspective; larger than 15 becomes unwieldy for discussion.

### Independence

IRS emphasizes independent directors (not compensated as employees or contractors, not
related to key employees, not receiving material financial benefits from the organization).
At minimum, a majority should be independent.

### Diversity

Composition should reflect the community served and bring diverse perspectives in:
demographic background, professional expertise (legal, financial, program, fundraising,
marketing, technology), lived experience, and geographic representation.

### Term Limits

Standard: 3-year terms, renewable for 2-3 consecutive terms. Benefits: ensures fresh
perspectives, prevents entrenchment, clarifies expectations. Some organizations opt for
staggered terms (1/3 of board rotates annually).

### Recruitment

Systematic process: identify skill and perspective gaps on current board → cultivate
prospective members → present clear expectations (give/get policy, meeting attendance,
committee service) → nominate → elect.

## Board Roles & Responsibilities

### Core Governance Functions (Chait, Holland & Taylor)

1. Set organizational direction (mission, vision, strategic priorities)
2. Ensure necessary resources (fundraising, resource development)
3. Provide oversight (fiscal oversight, program monitoring, CEO evaluation)

### Specific Responsibilities

- **Hire, support, and evaluate** the executive director/CEO (board's single most
  important responsibility)
- **Approve and monitor** the strategic plan and annual budget
- **Ensure financial integrity:** review financial statements, ensure audit, establish
  internal controls
- **Ensure legal and ethical integrity:** adopt and enforce key policies
- **Fundraise:** contribute personally and participate actively in resource development
- **Enhance the organization's public standing:** serve as ambassadors
- **Board self-assessment:** evaluate governance effectiveness regularly

## Board-Executive Director Relationship

### The Board Governs; The ED Manages

- **Board focus:** Mission, strategy, policy, fiscal oversight, ED evaluation
- **ED focus:** Day-to-day operations, program implementation, staff management,
  budget execution, board support

The most common governance pathology is board micromanagement. The second is board
disengagement. A healthy board-ED relationship maintains clear role boundaries while
ensuring robust communication and mutual accountability.

### ED Hiring Process

1. Board forms search committee
2. Develop position profile based on strategic needs
3. Solicit and screen candidates
4. Select finalist(s); conduct due diligence
5. Negotiate compensation and employment agreement
6. Announce and onboard

### ED Evaluation

Annual formal evaluation based on: progress against strategic goals, financial management,
board relations, staff development, external relations, and program impact. Use written goals
established at beginning of year. Link to compensation decisions.

### Succession Planning

Board responsibility. Maintain: emergency succession plan (who leads if ED is suddenly
unavailable), departure-defined succession plan (search process, transition management),
and ongoing leadership development pipeline.

## Committees

### Standing Committees

- **Executive Committee:** Acts between board meetings for urgent matters. Authority
  should be clearly limited in bylaws. Risk: can become de facto board, undermining
  full board engagement.
- **Finance Committee:** Oversees financial management, budgeting, audit, investment
  policies. Chair often serves as Treasurer.
- **Governance/Nominating Committee:** Leads board recruitment, orientation, assessment,
  and governance policy development.
- **Audit Committee:** Independent review of financial audits, internal controls. Should
  be composed of independent directors with financial literacy.

### Advisory Committees vs. Board Committees

Advisory committees may include non-board members and have no formal authority. Board
committees exercise board-delegated authority and should be board-member dominated.

### Committee Best Practices

- Clear written charters defining purpose, membership, authority
- Minutes maintained for all committee meetings
- Rotation of committee leadership
- Committees report to full board

## Board Meetings & Effectiveness

### Meeting Cadence

Minimum quarterly. Many boards meet bimonthly or monthly during critical periods.
Committee meetings between board meetings.

### Agenda Design

- **Consent agenda:** Routine items (minutes, committee reports, correspondence) voted
  as a block without discussion
- **Strategic discussion:** Substantial time allocated to forward-looking strategic topics
- **Board education:** Regular inclusion of program or mission-related education
- **Dashboard review:** Key metrics and dashboard indicators reviewed regularly

### Effective Board Meetings

- Distribute materials at least one week in advance
- Board chair and ED collaborate on agenda design
- Time allocation reflects priorities (spend more time on strategy, less on reports)
- Executive sessions (without staff present) scheduled regularly
- Minutes document decisions and action items

## Governance Policies

### Required/Recommended Policies

- **Conflict of Interest Policy:** Annual disclosure, recusal procedures. IRS Form 990,
  Part VI requires disclosure of whether the organization has such a policy and how it
  manages conflicts.
- **Whistleblower Policy:** Procedures for reporting suspected impropriety and
  protection from retaliation.
- **Document Retention and Destruction Policy:** Guidelines for retention schedules and
  destruction procedures, including electronic documents.
- **Joint Venture Policy:** If applicable, procedures for evaluating participation in
  joint ventures with for-profit entities.
- **Gift Acceptance Policy:** Types of gifts the organization will accept and procedures
  for review of non-standard gifts.
- **Investment Policy:** Prudent investor standards, asset allocation, socially
  responsible investing considerations.

## Board Development & Assessment

### Orientation

Comprehensive onboarding for new members: mission and history, programs, finances, board
member expectations, governance structure, site visits.

### Ongoing Education

Regular education sessions on: program content, financial literacy, fundraising, advocacy,
governance best practices, sector trends.

### Board Self-Assessment

Annual process assessing: mission and strategy, board composition and development,
board operations (meetings, committees, information), board-ED relationship, financial
oversight, fundraising effectiveness, and community ambassadorship.

### Board Giving Policy (Give/Get)

Establish clear expectations: each board member expected to make a personally significant
financial contribution AND participate in fundraising (get). Commonly: 100% board
participation in giving as a signal to other donors and funders.

## D&O Insurance

Directors & Officers liability insurance is essential. Covers: defense costs and
settlements for claims alleging wrongful acts in governance. Volunteer Protection Act
(1997) provides some protection for uncompensated volunteers but does not cover all
scenarios. State laws vary on director immunity.
