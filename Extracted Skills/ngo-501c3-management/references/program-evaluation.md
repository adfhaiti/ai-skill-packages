## Evaluation Fundamentals

### Purpose of Evaluation

Evaluation serves multiple purposes: accountability to funders and stakeholders,
organizational learning and program improvement, strategic decision-making,
demonstrating impact and building credibility, and contributing to sector knowledge.
The primary purpose should shape evaluation design.

### Formative vs. Summative Evaluation

- **Formative evaluation:** Conducted during program implementation to improve.
  Focuses on process, implementation fidelity, participant engagement. Audience:
  program staff and managers.
- **Summative evaluation:** Conducted after program maturity to assess outcomes
  and impact. Focuses on results, effectiveness, and value. Audience: funders,
  board, external stakeholders.

### Evaluation Standards (Joint Committee)

- **Utility:** Evaluation serves information needs of intended users
- **Feasibility:** Evaluation is realistic, prudent, diplomatic, and frugal
- **Propriety:** Evaluation is conducted legally, ethically, and with regard for
  those involved
- **Accuracy:** Evaluation reveals and conveys technically adequate information

## Logic Models & Theory of Change

### Logic Model Components

Standard components, read left to right:

**Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact**

- **Inputs:** Resources invested (staff, funding, facilities, materials, partners)
- **Activities:** What the program does (services, trainings, events, interventions)
- **Outputs:** Direct products of activities (number served, sessions delivered,
  materials distributed) — these are NOT outcomes
- **Outcomes:** Changes in participants or conditions — short-term (learning,
  awareness, attitudes), intermediate (behavior change, practice adoption),
  long-term (conditions improved, systems changed)
- **Impact:** Ultimate, sustained change at population or system level

### Developing a Logic Model

1. Start from the end: what ultimate change does the program seek?
2. Work backward: what outcomes must occur to reach impact?
3. Specify outputs that demonstrate activities occurred as intended
4. Define activities that produce those outputs
5. Identify inputs required to conduct activities
6. State assumptions at each link

### Common Pitfalls

- Confusing outputs with outcomes ("served 500 clients" is an output; "clients gained
  employment" is an outcome)
- Overclaiming: attributing outcomes to a single program when many factors contribute
- Under-specifying: "improved well-being" without defining what well-being means in
  measurable terms
- Missing assumptions: not surfacing the conditions that must hold true for the logic
  to work

## Evaluation Design

### Design Options

- **Experimental (RCT):** Random assignment to treatment and control. Strongest causal
  evidence. Often impractical or unethical in community settings.
- **Quasi-experimental:** Comparison group without randomization. Requires statistical
  controls for selection bias. More feasible for most nonprofits.
- **Pre-post:** Measure before and after intervention. No comparison group — cannot
  rule out alternative explanations for change.
- **Post-only:** Measure outcomes only after intervention. Weakest design; use only
  when baseline data is impossible and outcomes are clearly attributable.
- **Mixed methods:** Quantitative (surveys, administrative data) + qualitative
  (interviews, focus groups, case studies). Recommended for most nonprofit evaluations.

### Choosing Design Strength

Match design rigor to: evaluation purpose (learning vs. accountability), program
maturity (pilot vs. established), available resources, and stakeholder evidence
requirements. A well-executed quasi-experimental design is better than a poorly
executed RCT.

### Key Evaluation Questions

- **Process:** Was the program implemented as intended? Who participated? What was
  the dosage?
- **Outcome:** Did participants change in the expected ways? How much change?
- **Attribution:** To what extent can changes be attributed to the program?
- **Context:** Under what conditions does the program work? For whom?
- **Value:** Were the outcomes worth the investment? Compared to alternatives?

## Data Collection Methods

### Quantitative Methods

- **Surveys:** Standardized instruments; use validated scales when available. Be
  mindful of response burden and literacy levels.
- **Administrative data:** Program records, attendance, service utilization.
  Low cost but limited to what's already collected.
- **Standardized assessments:** External instruments for measuring specific
  constructs (depression scales, academic achievement tests).
- **Observation checklists:** Structured observation with defined indicators.

### Qualitative Methods

- **Interviews:** Semi-structured, in-depth exploration of participant experience
  and program mechanisms
- **Focus groups:** Group discussion for exploring shared experiences, consensus,
  and divergence
- **Case studies:** In-depth examination of representative or extreme cases to
  understand how and why outcomes occur
- **Document review:** Program materials, participant work products, meeting notes

### Participatory Approaches

Involve program participants and community members in evaluation design, data
collection, and interpretation. Increases relevance, builds evaluation capacity
in community, and often produces more actionable findings.

## Outcome Measurement

### Developing Indicators

For each outcome: specify the indicator (what will be measured), data source,
collection method, collection frequency, target (what constitutes success), and
responsible party.

**SMART indicators:** Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.

### Selecting Measurement Tools

- Use validated instruments when available (established reliability and validity)
- Adapt existing tools rather than building from scratch
- If developing new tools: pilot test, assess reliability, refine
- Balance rigor with respondent burden — shorter surveys have higher completion rates

### Meaningful Significance

Statistical significance (p-values) answers: "Is this result likely due to chance?"
Meaningful significance answers: "Does this magnitude of change matter in people's
lives?" Report effect sizes and practical significance alongside statistical tests.
A small, statistically significant effect may be meaningless in practice. A large,
practically important effect may be non-significant in small samples.

## Utilization-Focused Evaluation

### Patton's Framework

Evaluation should be judged by its utility and actual use. Key principles:

1. Identify intended users and their information needs before designing evaluation
2. Involve intended users throughout the evaluation process to build ownership
3. Focus evaluation questions on decisions users actually face
4. Produce timely, accessible findings in formats tailored to users
5. Process use matters — participation in evaluation builds evaluative thinking

### Evaluation Use

Distinguish: instrumental use (direct decision-making based on findings), conceptual
use (changing how people think about the program), process use (learning through
participation in evaluation), and symbolic use (using evaluation to legitimize
decisions already made — avoid this).

## Reporting & Communication

### Audiences and Formats

- **Board:** Executive summary, dashboard of key indicators, trend analysis
- **Funders:** Full report aligned with grant requirements, narrative plus data
- **Staff:** Actionable findings with clear recommendations, facilitated discussion
- **Participants/Community:** Accessible summaries, visual formats, community
  presentations
- **Public:** Website, annual report infographics, social media

### Effective Reporting

- Start with key findings, not methodology
- Use data visualization: dashboards, charts, maps — most people process visual
  information faster than tables
- Include participant voice: quotes, stories alongside data
- Be honest about limitations and mixed findings — credibility requires transparency
- Distinguish between data (what was measured), findings (what the data show), and
  interpretation (what the findings mean)

## Building Evaluation Capacity

### Organizational Evaluation Maturity

**Stage 1 — Minimal:** Ad hoc data collection for grant reporting; no systematic
evaluation.

**Stage 2 — Emerging:** Some staff with evaluation training; basic logic models;
outputs tracked.

**Stage 3 — Established:** Evaluation integrated into program design; outcome data
collected; staff evaluation competencies developed.

**Stage 4 — Advanced:** Organization-wide evaluative thinking; multi-method evaluation;
evaluation informs strategy; external dissemination of findings.

Progress through stages by: investing in staff evaluation skills, designating
evaluation responsibility, building data systems, creating time and budget for
evaluation, and modeling data-informed decision-making from leadership.

### Evaluation Budget

Allocate 5-10% of program budget to evaluation. More for new, innovative, or
scaling programs. Less for mature, evidence-based programs with established
tracking systems. Include: evaluation staff time, external evaluator/consultant,
data collection costs (survey platforms, incentives, translation), data analysis
software, and dissemination.
